## My Context Before I Begin

I am evaluating this as the person who signs the purchase order, defends the spend to the Dean, presents it to the ethics committee, and then fields complaints from 30+ PG students when the tool doesn't work as promised.

I have a departmental software budget of ₹5–8 lakhs per year. We already pay for institutional PubMed access, a Turnitin license, and basic statistical software. Every rupee I spend on a new tool is either justified by measurable outcome improvement — specifically, indexed publications and thesis acceptance rates — or it is money I cannot recover and a conversation with the Dean I do not want to have.

My department's publication rate is tracked by NAAC. It directly affects our accreditation score. My 30+ PG students produce theses of widely varying quality. The bottleneck is not their intelligence — they are well-selected. The bottleneck is that nobody has taught them systematic research methodology, and I do not have bandwidth to teach it one student at a time.

I need a tool that works at department scale, survives an ethics committee review, produces measurable publication outcomes, and comes from a vendor I can trust to still be operating when I renew next year.

I am not moved by narrative copywriting. I am moved by evidence. Let me evaluate what I found.

### COPY A — Schwartz-Halbert Framework
*"Your Clinical Training Had a Supervisor Standing Next to You. Your Thesis Has a Deadline."*

**1. Institutional Value Proposition: 4/10**

This copy was not written for me. It was written for the student sitting in the hostel room at 11 PM. The emotional identification with the PG student experience is well-executed — the 400 PubMed results, the blank Word document, the ChatGPT panic — but I am not that person. I am the person managing 30 of those people simultaneously.

There is one institutional section buried late — a single testimonial from an Associate Professor who appreciated the ethics design. That is the only moment where this copy acknowledges my existence. One paragraph in a 3,800-word document. That is not enough.

**2. ROI Clarity: 3/10**

The ROI math presented is entirely individual-student ROI: ₹3,000 against a career publication, time saved on literature search. There is no institutional ROI framing. I need to answer: what is my cost per publication if I equip 30 students with this tool for one academic year? What does that cost versus the cost of those students missing publication targets? Copy A does not help me build that case.

**3. Academic Integrity: 8/10**

This is the strongest section of the copy. The Learn Mode description is detailed and credible. The explanation of why the tool explicitly does not write for students — and the claim that it is defensible to a university ethics committee — is exactly the language I need. The FAQ on AI generation is honest and sophisticated. If I were presenting to my ethics committee, I could quote this section directly. Score is not 10 because the integrity argument is mixed into student-facing content rather than being surfaced for institutional audiences.

**4. Scalability: 2/10**

The word "department" appears rarely. There is no mention of multi-user management, supervisor dashboards, or how this works when 30 students are running simultaneous projects. The institutional offering section in Copy B handles this; Copy A does not attempt it. I cannot evaluate fit for 30 students from this copy.

**5. Publication Impact: 5/10**

The feature descriptions are detailed and credible — PRISMA 2020, triple-agent screening, RoB 2, Vancouver citations. These are real methodological features that would genuinely improve thesis and manuscript quality. But the copy frames impact entirely around the individual student's first paper, not around a department's aggregate publication output. I need to see something like "departments using ScholarSync increased indexed publications by X%." This copy does not provide that.

**6. Vendor Credibility: 6/10**

The technical specificity is credible — MeSH-optimised Boolean queries, Reciprocal Rank Fusion, Cohere Rerank, HyDE retrieval. These are not marketing terms invented to impress. Someone who knows what they are doing built this. The testimonials reference specific institutions (NIMS Hyderabad, AIIMS Bhopal, BJMC Pune, NIMHANS Bengaluru). The NMC-awareness claim is appropriate. But there is no company history, no team description, no indication of how long this product has existed, no SLA or data security statement. For institutional procurement, vendor credibility requires more than product credibility.

**7. Would I Buy Institutional Licenses? No.**

Not based on this copy alone. This is an excellent student-facing sales document. It would make a PG student subscribe. It does not give me what I need to justify institutional spend. I would need to find the institutional page myself — the copy does not lead me there.

**What Is Missing:**
- A supervisor dashboard or visibility-into-student-progress feature surfaced prominently
- Institutional ROI framing (cost per publication, comparison to current departmental spend)
- Data security and privacy statement (student research data residency)
- Vendor stability indicators
- Any metric about publication outcomes at the department level

### COPY B — Ogilvy-Hopkins Framework
*"A physician in Raipur now has the same research infrastructure as a physician at Harvard Medical School."*

**1. Institutional Value Proposition: 7/10**

This is meaningfully better for my purposes. Section 8 — the Institutional Offering — speaks directly to department heads. The language is precise: "A multi-user dashboard from which you see every active research project in your department." "Supervisor visibility tools that show you where students are in the process before the first draft arrives on your desk." That sentence alone is worth more than the entire emotional arc of Copy A from my perspective. Catching a methodological error in week two instead of week eight is a real problem this copy names accurately. The institutional section is underdeveloped — it is one page in a long document — but it exists and it says the right things.

**2. ROI Clarity: 7/10**

The comparison table is genuinely useful for procurement justification. Specific time savings: manual Boolean query construction (45–90 minutes for a trained researcher, 3+ hours for a student) versus 2 minutes automated. PRISMA flow diagram (2–4 hours manually, zero with ScholarSync). Covidence comparison: $160 USD per month for individual researchers versus ₹1,999 for a tool that also includes writing, citation management, evidence classification, and an editor. I can use this table in a Dean presentation. The comparison to institutional tools (Covidence) at real prices is exactly the ROI framing I need. My weakness: there is still no per-seat institutional pricing, so I cannot actually calculate my annual cost from this page.

**3. Academic Integrity: 8/10**

The institutional ethics argument is explicit: "Every tool you recommend to students carries your professional endorsement of the research it produces." This is written for me, not for the student. The explanation of Learn Mode — that the AI never produces text on the student's behalf, that it asks questions and teaches structure — is the ethics committee answer. "This is defensible to an ethics committee. It is the answer to the question your dean will ask when AI in academic writing comes before the board." That is precise targeting. I know exactly what meeting that sentence is written for.

**4. Scalability: 7/10**

This copy specifically addresses multi-user management, per-student usage analytics, and supervisor visibility into project progress. It names the institutional offering explicitly and invites contact for per-seat pricing. This is the only copy that adequately addresses my operational need to manage 30+ students simultaneously. It is still thin — I would want to know how the dashboard actually works — but the acknowledgment of the scalability problem is present and accurate.

**5. Publication Impact: 6/10**

The time savings per task are credible and specific. 60–80 hours of infrastructure work reduced to under 10 hours for a systematic review. Manual abstract screening: 40+ hours per 1,000 citations versus minutes for AI screening. I can translate this into publication timeline improvements. What I still lack: any data showing that departments using ScholarSync actually publish more, or that manuscripts produced with ScholarSync have higher acceptance rates. These are outcome claims I need, and no copy makes them with evidence.

**6. Vendor Credibility: 7/10**

The factual proof block at the beginning — ten numbered facts stated as facts, not as marketing claims — establishes credibility before I have read anything emotional. The methodology vocabulary (Cochrane RoB 2, PRISMA 2020, Reciprocal Rank Fusion, ProseMirror, HyDE) is accurate and demonstrates domain knowledge. The invitation to contact for institutional pricing implies a real business relationship, not a consumer product. This is the most professional-feeling copy for a B2B buyer.

**7. Would I Buy Institutional Licenses? Conditionally Yes.**

I would contact them for institutional pricing based on this copy. I would not sign without a demo, a data security review, and actual pricing. But this copy gives me enough to start the conversation and enough to brief the Dean on what the product does. It is the only copy that speaks to my actual decision-making context rather than a student's thesis panic.

**What Is Missing:**
- Publication outcome data from departments (even directional)
- Data security and residency statement (I will be asked by IT)
- Any pricing indication for institutional seats (even a range)
- Implementation support or onboarding description
- Minimum seat count or contract duration expectations

### COPY C — Wiebe-Kennedy Framework
*"11 PM. 47 browser tabs. A blank Word document."*

**1. Institutional Value Proposition: 3/10**

This copy opens with a scene I recognise — but it is not my scene. It is my student's scene. The copy is tightly, deliberately focused on one buyer persona: "the MD/MS resident required to publish before graduation." The "Not For Everyone" section explicitly names this as a student-facing product. The Institutional tier exists in the pricing table — one line, "Custom" — with a brief description that mentions multi-user management and supervisor visibility tools. That is the entirety of the institutional address. It is insufficient for my evaluation.

**2. ROI Clarity: 4/10**

The cost comparisons are present — Covidence at $3,500 per review (this number is wrong by the way; Covidence is priced per review at roughly $148 for researchers, not $3,500 — this damages credibility), PubMed and Zotero as free tools that cost time. The framing of "what would you pay to cut literature search from three weeks to two hours" is a useful rhetorical device but it is directed at the student calculating personal time value, not at me calculating departmental cost-per-publication. No institutional ROI. No department-level numbers.

**3. Academic Integrity: 7/10**

The Learn Mode description is clear and accurate. The ethics framing in the "Not For Everyone" section and the micro-FAQ is well-handled. "Learn Mode was designed to be defensible to an academic integrity committee — it teaches, not writes." That is the right sentence. But it appears in a micro-FAQ at the bottom, not surfaced as a key institutional selling point.

**4. Scalability: 2/10**

One line in the pricing table. No elaboration. I cannot evaluate how 30 students would be managed, whether supervisors have visibility, what happens when students have conflicting search priorities, or whether the platform has any multi-user coordination features beyond existence. This copy was not written for me.

**5. Publication Impact: 5/10**

The feature descriptions are detailed enough to suggest genuine publication impact — PRISMA compliance, triple-agent screening, Vancouver citations, RoB 2. But the framing is entirely around the student's first paper. "The residents who publish on time started their workflow first." Correct observation. Not a departmental publication strategy.

**6. Vendor Credibility: 5/10**

The Covidence pricing error is a credibility problem. A buyer who knows Covidence pricing — which any department head who has evaluated systematic review tools would — will notice that $3,500 per review is wrong by an order of magnitude. Getting a competitor's pricing wrong in your copy signals either carelessness or deliberate misrepresentation. Neither builds trust with a procurement decision-maker. The rest of the product description is credible, but this error costs points.

**7. Would I Buy Institutional Licenses? No.**

This copy does not have enough institutional content for me to make a case to the Dean. The Covidence pricing error would undermine my credibility if I used this as a reference. I would need to find significantly more information before proceeding.

**What Is Missing:**
- Everything Copy B includes in its institutional section
- Accurate competitive pricing (the Covidence figure is wrong)
- Any department-level outcome framing
- Data security and IT compliance information

### COPY D — Deep Work Framework
*"Research is deep work. Your tools are designed for shallow."*

**1. Institutional Value Proposition: 5/10**

This copy frames the problem at a level of abstraction that is intellectually interesting — attention residue, chronic partial attention, cognitive switching costs — but does not translate that intellectual framework into operational institutional language. The institutional pricing section contains one genuinely useful sentence: "The metric that matters for institutional adoption: what does it cost to have researchers working in fragmented, attention-taxing toolchains, producing work of lower quality, requiring more supervisor revision time, and building less durable research capability than they would in a unified environment?" That question is valid and important. But it is asked without any attempt to answer it. The cost of the current fragmented workflow in my department is real — but this copy does not help me quantify it.

**2. ROI Clarity: 4/10**

The Newport framework is intellectually credible. The attention residue argument is actually well-suited for an institutional buyer who manages researcher productivity. But the ROI framing is qualitative throughout — "quality ceiling rises," "cognitive resources," "compound advantage." I cannot present a qualitative argument to a Dean who wants numbers. The absence of a comparison table (which Copy B provides) is a significant gap. Qualitative ROI arguments require quantitative backup that this copy does not provide.

**3. Academic Integrity: 8/10**

The Learn Mode section in this copy is the most philosophically rigorous treatment of the AI-skill-versus-delegation question across all five copies. The Newport-Ericsson deliberate practice framing — that Learn Mode builds research capacity rather than substituting for it — is the most defensible ethics argument I have read. "The distinction between a tool that makes mediocre research faster and a tool that makes excellent research possible is not a marketing distinction. It is the entire question." I would use this framing in an ethics committee presentation. This section is genuinely valuable.

**4. Scalability: 4/10**

The institutional section mentions supervisor visibility and multi-user management. It is thin — about as thin as Copy B's institutional section, but without Copy B's specificity about what the supervisor dashboard actually shows. The theoretical framing about fragmented toolchains is relevant at department scale but is not operationalised into concrete features I can evaluate.

**5. Publication Impact: 5/10**

The compound advantage section is interesting — the long-term trajectory argument that deep research practice builds compounding capability. As a department head who is trying to build a research culture across a five-year horizon, not just get through this year's thesis submissions, this argument resonates more than the "publish your first paper" framing of other copies. But it is still qualitative. I need publication outcome data.

**6. Vendor Credibility: 7/10**

The authority section (Section 6) is strong — demonstrating credibility through methodological specificity rather than claiming it. PRISMA 2020, RoB 2, MeSH, Vancouver, triple-agent screening. The absence of exclamation marks and urgency tactics is appropriate for the serious institutional buyer. The tone is peer-level, not sales-level. This copy would appeal to faculty colleagues who I might consult before making this decision. The Newport intellectual framework will resonate with researchers who have read him. That is a real subset of my department.

**7. Would I Buy Institutional Licenses? No, but I would invite a demo.**

This copy would make me take the vendor seriously as intellectually credible. It would not give me enough to write a procurement proposal. The qualitative arguments need quantitative backing that is absent. But I would feel comfortable having a conversation with whoever wrote this — they seem to understand what good research looks like.

**What Is Missing:**
- Any quantitative departmental outcome data
- Concrete description of supervisor dashboard features
- Institutional pricing structure
- The practical operational details that a department head needs alongside the intellectual framework

### COPY E — Collier Emotional Arc
*"You need to write a research paper. And you're not sure you know how."*

Copy E is the most emotionally sophisticated of the five, and the most tightly written. The victory scene — the department head reading the systematic review, looking up, saying "This is thorough work" — is the only moment across all five copies where a department head is placed as a protagonist rather than a background character. That is a significant insight. But the institutional pricing section remains thin: 40 faculty, 200 students, eight projects — it names the scale problem without addressing the management solution in any detail. There is one good sentence in the institutional section: "Learn Mode means you can recommend this to your department ethics committee. The supervisor dashboard means you can see where students are in the process before the draft meeting — not after." That is useful. It needed to be a page, not two sentences.

**2. ROI Clarity: 4/10**

"ScholarSync researchers average 18 days for a complete literature review. That's not a marketing claim." It is, however, stated without evidence. I need to know how that number was derived. Was it a user survey? Internal analytics? Compared to what baseline? "Average MD literature review takes four months with manual methods" — this is also stated as fact without citation. I want to believe these numbers. I cannot use them in a Dean presentation without supporting evidence. The career-value framing ("one indexed publication on your CV at the time of PG entrance changes your percentile") is accurate and would resonate with students, but does not help me build the institutional business case.

**3. Academic Integrity: 8/10**

The Learn Mode ethical positioning is strong across all copies; Copy E states it most cleanly for an institutional recommender: "Institutions can recommend Learn Mode to an academic integrity committee without hesitation. It is the product's moral backbone, and it was designed that way deliberately." That is precisely the language I would use with my ethics committee. The "invisible curriculum" framing in Section 3 — naming the structural absence of research methodology teaching in Indian medical education — is the most accurate diagnosis of the problem I face in my department. It is well-written and it is true.

**4. Scalability: 4/10**

Similar to Copy D — the institutional section acknowledges the scale problem but does not operationalise the solution. "One research standard, applied consistently, with visibility into every project" is the right value proposition. It needed operational detail: what does the supervisor dashboard show? **5. Publication Impact: 6/10**

The "18 days versus four months" claim — even unsubstantiated — is the most concrete publication timeline impact across any of the five copies. If that number is real and can be cited, it is the single most powerful institutional sales claim in the entire corpus. A department head who can show the Dean that tool adoption cut median thesis completion time by X months would have a very strong procurement case. This copy makes the claim. It does not support it.

**6. Vendor Credibility: 6/10**

The institutional social proof — AIIMS, JIPMER, NIMHANS, NIMS, Sri Ramachandra — is the strongest across all five copies. Named institutions with named users (Dr. Rohit Sharma, Dr. Priya Menon, Dr. Arjun Mehta, Dr. Priya Nair) add specificity. The testimonials are well-written and feel real. The institutional professor quote — "I finally have an AI tool I can recommend to my students without worrying about integrity violations" — is exactly the testimonial I needed and it appears in the right copy. But the "18 days" claim, unsubstantiated, creates a credibility problem. I would rather have a supported smaller number than an unsupported large one.

**7. Would I Buy Institutional Licenses? Possibly, pending follow-up.**

If I could verify the "18 days" claim with actual data and get a concrete institutional pricing proposal, this copy would give me enough emotional and practical ammunition to advocate internally. The victory scene framing — I, the department head, receive "This is thorough work" — positions the tool's success as my success, which is the correct motivational frame for a departmental buyer. But I need numbers behind the claims.

**What Is Missing:**
- Evidence backing the "18 days" and "four months" comparative timeline claims
- Supervisor dashboard feature details
- IT/data security information
- Institutional pricing range
- A clearer description of what "one research standard applied consistently" actually means operationally

## The One Addition That Would Make Me Sign a Purchase Order

**I would add the following to Copy B, in the Institutional section:**

A one-page institutional case study from a named Indian medical department showing: number of students, pre-ScholarSync annual indexed publications, post-ScholarSync annual indexed publications, and department head's name.

Not a testimonial quote. A structured case study with numbers.

If that case study showed, for example, that a department of 35 PG students went from 8 indexed publications per year to 14 indexed publications per year over two academic years — with a per-student license cost of, say, ₹18,000 per year per student across 30 students at ₹5.4 lakhs total — then I have a cost-per-additional-publication number. In that hypothetical example: ₹5.4 lakhs to produce 6 additional indexed publications = ₹90,000 per publication. I can compare that to the cost of one missed NAAC publication target.

That number — whatever it actually is — is the number that ends the procurement discussion.

Every other objection I have — ethics committee, student adoption, IT security — is manageable. The objection I cannot manage without data is: will this measurably improve my department's publication output? Give me one real department's numbers, and I sign.

## Observations Across All Five Copies

**What all five copies do well:**
- The Learn Mode ethics design is consistently described with enough specificity to be credible and defensible
- The technical methodology vocabulary (PRISMA 2020, RoB 2, MeSH, Vancouver, triple-agent screening) is accurate throughout and demonstrates genuine domain knowledge
- The NMC and Indian medical education context is appropriate and specific
- The competitive comparison to Covidence is relevant and directionally correct (though Copy C gets the pricing wrong)
- The database coverage (PubMed + Semantic Scholar + OpenAlex) is a genuine differentiator described consistently

**What all five copies lack for institutional procurement:**
- Publication outcome data from existing institutional customers
- Data security and privacy statement (where does student research data live? who owns it? what happens if the company closes?)
- Vendor stability indicators (founding date, team size, funding, how long the product has existed)
- SLA or uptime guarantee for an institutional deployment
- Implementation and onboarding description for department-scale adoption
- IT integration details (SSO, LMS integration, institutional email domains)
- A structured institutional pricing page rather than "contact us"
- Named reference customers in India willing to speak with prospective buyers

**My overall assessment of the product:**

The product described in these copies, if it works as described, solves a real and measurable problem in my department. The Learn Mode design is genuinely differentiated — I have not seen another tool that is architecturally opposed to writing for students rather than architecturally optimised for it. The PRISMA systematic review infrastructure addresses the exact gap between what my students are asked to produce and what they know how to build. The Vancouver citation export and NMC-awareness are appropriate for our context.

If I could verify the product functions as described, get a data security review, see one institutional case study with publication outcome data, and receive an institutional pricing proposal in the ₹3–5 lakh annual range for 30+ seats, I would be a buyer.

The copies, as currently written, do not get me there. Copy B comes closest.
