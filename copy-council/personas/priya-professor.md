## My Position Before Reading Any of These

I need to be transparent about where I am coming from before I score anything.

This year alone, I have caught three students submitting ChatGPT-written methods sections. Not suspected — caught. I know the tells: the generic passive constructions, the hallucinated citations, the suspiciously confident tone in students who cannot answer basic questions about their own methodology in the viva voce. I had a student in July who submitted a systematic review with a PRISMA flow diagram that did not match the number of papers in her reference list. When I asked her to explain the discrepancy, she could not. She had not read the diagram she submitted.

I am not anti-technology. I use PubMed every day. I have been teaching evidence-based medicine for fourteen years. I understand what good research infrastructure looks like, and I understand why students struggle — the system genuinely does not teach them. I have empathy for the students. I have zero tolerance for tools that make the problem worse by doing the thinking for them.

When someone tells me they have an "AI research tool," my immediate assumption is that it is another way for students to generate plausible-sounding garbage they do not understand. Changing my mind requires specific evidence, not marketing language.

With that said: I read all five copies. Here is my honest reaction to each.

## Copy A — Schwartz-Halbert Framework

### First Impression
This is the most comprehensive copy of the five. It reads like something that was actually written for medical researchers, not repurposed from a general SaaS template. The specificity is what gets my attention first — MeSH-tagged Boolean queries, Reciprocal Rank Fusion, PRISMA 2020, RoB 2, Oxford CEBM hierarchy. Someone in this company knows what they are talking about, or has spoken at length to someone who does.

The problem-agitation section describing the student's first week — the 400 PubMed results, the downloads folder they are afraid to open, the blank Word document — is accurate. I have sat across from this student 40 times. That said, the section on the student opening ChatGPT and getting hallucinated citations gave me a complicated reaction. On one hand, it is honest and accurate. On the other hand, I am suspicious of any tool that positions itself as the ethical alternative to ChatGPT while also offering features like "AI humanisation" (Pro plan). If I am reading this for my department, that feature in the fine print concerns me. What is humanisation for, if not to make AI-generated text less detectable?

### Learn Mode
The three-law articulation is the single most promising thing I have read in any of these copies. "Never write for you. Socratic first, direct instruction second. Evidence is sacred." If this is actually how the product works — not just a marketing claim — this is defensible. The testimonial from the Associate Professor at BJMC Pune about watching the AI refuse to write a paragraph and redirect the student with three questions landed for me. That is the behaviour I would need to see demonstrated before I trust the claim, but the claim itself is the right claim to make.

### Supervisory Value
Weak. The institutional section in Copy A mentions an "institutional plan" in the pricing briefly, and the BJMC Professor testimonial is the only moment where a supervisor's perspective appears. The FAQ addresses students almost exclusively. If I am the person who recommends or blocks this tool for my department, I need the copy to speak to me directly about what supervision looks like with this tool. How does it change my supervisory workload? Can I see what my students are doing? Does it give me any visibility into the quality of their literature search before they walk into my office with a draft built on a flawed foundation?

### The "AI Humanisation" Problem
I need to address this directly. The Pro plan mentions "AI humanisation — light, medium, heavy." In the context of the FAQ on AI detection, the copy tries to frame this as "making your writing more distinctly yours." I do not buy that framing. Humanisation is for one purpose in academic contexts: making AI-generated text harder to detect. The copy cannot simultaneously claim the moral high ground on academic integrity and offer a feature designed to help students evade detection tools. That contradiction, if a colleague raises it in a department meeting, kills the institutional recommendation.

## Copy B — Ogilvy-Hopkins Framework

### First Impression
This copy speaks to me in a way none of the others quite do, because it opens with a structural argument I agree with: the quality gap in Indian medical research is infrastructural, not intellectual. I have been making this argument in faculty meetings for eight years. The access parity framing — a physician in Raipur deserving the same research infrastructure as a physician at Harvard — is not just marketing. It is a legitimate equity argument that I find persuasive.

The factual proof block is useful. Numbered, specific, methodologically grounded. I can read point 6 — the triple-agent AI screening described as "citable in a systematic review's Methods section" — and immediately know whether that claim would survive scrutiny in my department (it would, if the methodology is implemented as described). The claim that RoB 2 output is "auditable" and "can be included in supplementary materials" is the kind of language that a researcher reads differently than a student. It is speaking to me.

### The Demonstration Section
The SGLT2 inhibitor search demonstration is exceptional. It shows me exactly what the tool does, in the language of evidence synthesis — DAPA-HF, EMPEROR-Reduced, I-squared heterogeneity, the citation graph traversal finding papers the keyword search missed. A student reading this would understand what the tool does. A faculty member reading this would think: "if this is accurate, this is genuinely useful." The comparison to a medical librarian doing three hours of work in eleven seconds is the right framing — it describes a productivity benefit without claiming the AI is doing the researcher's intellectual work.

### Supervisory Value
The institutional offering section is the best supervisor-facing content in all five copies. "For department heads and research supervisors" is a direct address to my role. The multi-user dashboard, the supervisor visibility tools, the ability to see where students are in the process before the first draft arrives on my desk — this is addressing a real pain point. I currently find out about methodological errors at week eight, after the student has built an entire manuscript on a flawed literature search. The framing "the difference between catching a methodological error in week two and catching it in week eight" is exactly right.

The ethics committee positioning in this section is also good: "This is defensible to an ethics committee. It is the answer to the question your dean will ask when AI in academic writing comes before the board." I am that person. My dean will ask that question. This copy is the only one that anticipates it and provides a specific answer.

### What is Missing
The comparison table between current workflow and ScholarSync is useful, but I notice it does not address the humanisation feature. Like Copy A, the Pro plan mentions it. Unlike Copy A, Copy B does not draw attention to it in the ethics section — which means it is still there as a liability but less visible. I am not reassured by invisibility; I am more concerned by it.

The copy also does not tell me enough about what Learn Mode's Socratic interaction actually looks like in practice. The institutional section trusts me to believe the claim without demonstration. Copy A and Copy E both give more concrete examples of the Learn Mode conversation. For a supervisor deciding whether to recommend this to 10 students, the demonstration matters more than the description.

## Copy D — Deep Work Framework

### First Impression
I respect the intellectual ambition here. The Newport framework, the attention residue research, the Ericsson deliberate practice citation — this copy is clearly written for someone who reads academic work and responds to theoretical frameworks. In another context, I would find this copy compelling.

The problem is that it is not written for me. It is written for the version of me that has two hours on a Sunday to read a thoughtful essay about cognitive science and academic productivity. The version of me that is marking eight student drafts, running afternoon OPD, preparing lectures for the undergraduate batch, and sitting on the hospital ethics committee has approximately three minutes to decide whether I want to recommend a tool to my students. This copy requires sustained attention to extract its value proposition. That is a structural problem.

### The Learn Mode Section (Section 4)
This is the most philosophically complete treatment of Learn Mode in any of the five copies. The distinction between "becoming a better researcher" and "becoming a more efficient consumer of AI output" is the correct distinction. The skill atrophy argument — the neural circuit deterioration through disuse — is accurate and important. I have seen this happen. I have postdoctoral fellows who cannot construct a search strategy from scratch because they have been using Covidence's automated tools for four years.

The five-year trajectory comparison is particularly effective for me as a supervisor. I think about my students' careers over time, not just their current thesis. A student who uses Learn Mode for five papers and a student who uses AI-generated text for five papers are going to have different capabilities in year ten of their careers, and this copy is the only one that makes that argument explicitly.

But this section is buried in a long copy that most of my students, and most of my colleagues, will not read to the end.

### The Compound Advantage Section
Outstanding content, wrong audience positioning. This would be a superb piece of content for a faculty newsletter, a CME talk on AI in medical education, or an institutional recommendation paper. As a landing page section for time-pressured residents and supervisors, it is the right argument in the wrong format.

### Supervisory Value
The institutional pricing section mentions "supervisor visibility into research workflow" and correctly frames the institutional ROI question: "what does it cost to have researchers working in fragmented, attention-taxing toolchains, producing work of lower quality, requiring more supervisor revision time?" That is a legitimate framing. But it is three sentences in a 4,000-word document. The supervisor is an afterthought in the architecture of this copy, despite being the institutional decision-maker.

## Copy E — Collier Emotional Arc

### First Impression
This copy made me uncomfortable in the first section, and then surprised me by the middle. The 11 PM hostel room scene is vivid and accurate — I know my students in that room, I know the draft-and-delete WhatsApp message, I know the folder called "Research" with 23 papers and six read properly. The emotional accuracy is real.

What made me uncomfortable: this copy is primarily an emotional appeal, and emotional appeals for research tools in academic medicine make me cautious. Students do not need to feel better about their research process. They need to understand it. But I will admit the emotional arc here is more sophisticated than I initially gave it credit for.

### The Invisible Curriculum Section
This section landed. "They didn't know either. They figured it out the hard way." This is true. I figured it out the hard way. My own guide's method of supervision was to return my drafts with red marks and no explanation of how to fix them. The framing that research skills are learnable — PICO, MeSH, PRISMA, evidence synthesis — "not gifts but skills that can be taught" is the correct pedagogical position, and this copy states it most clearly of all five.

### The Victory Scene (Section 7)
I want to be honest about my reaction to this section. My first instinct was that it is manipulative — a fabricated emotional scene designed to sell a subscription. My second reaction, after sitting with it, is that the scene is pedagogically accurate. A student who has built a complete, methodologically rigorous systematic review in 18 days instead of five months — that student does walk away from the guide meeting differently. That moment is real. And if the tool produces that outcome, the emotional appeal is justified.

The detail that matters to me: "you know the afternoon in Learn Mode when you were stuck on your Discussion section and the AI asked you: 'What do you want the reader to walk away believing?' And you thought about it — actually thought about it — and then you wrote the answer yourself." That is the pedagogically correct sequence. The AI as question-asker, not answer-provider. If that is what the tool actually does, this is an honest emotional appeal, not a manipulative one.

### The Supervisor Testimonial
The quote attributed to Dr. Priya Nair, Associate Professor, Sri Ramachandra Institute, Chennai, is the most useful testimonial in any of the five copies from my perspective: "Learn Mode is exactly what I've needed. My students arrive at their first draft meetings with structurally sound work. The AI taught them — it didn't write for them. That distinction matters to this department."

That is exactly what I would say if the tool worked as advertised. It is also exactly what I would need to be able to say to my department head before recommending it. However — and this is important — the testimonial is presented among student testimonials, not in a dedicated supervisor section. The institutional buyer's perspective is buried among social proof rather than elevated as a primary selling point.

### Institutional Section
"ScholarSync gives your department one research standard, applied consistently, with visibility into every project. Learn Mode means you can recommend this to your department ethics committee. The supervisor dashboard means you can see where students are in the process before the draft meeting — not after."

This is good. It is the clearest articulation in Copy E of the supervisory value proposition. But it is in the pricing section, under Institutional Plan, in a few sentences. Compared to what Copy B does with the institutional section, this is an afterthought.

## Full Ranking: Best to Worst for My Purposes

### 1. Copy B (Ogilvy-Hopkins) — Best for Institutional Decision-Making

This is the copy I would share with my department head. The access parity argument is compelling at the institutional level. The supervisor dashboard section directly addresses my supervisory concerns. The SGLT2 demonstration gives me enough evidence to trust the methodology claims. The ethics committee framing in the institutional section gives me the language I would need to defend this recommendation.

It is not perfect — the humanisation feature is still a liability, and Learn Mode is described rather than demonstrated — but it is the most complete case for institutional adoption.

### 2. Copy A (Schwartz-Halbert) — Best Methodology; Humanisation Problem

Copy A has the deepest methodological content and the most complete FAQ. The three laws of Learn Mode are the clearest product articulation of any copy. The testimonial from the BJMC Pune professor is the strongest supervisor-voice moment in Copy A. I would score this first if not for the humanisation feature contradiction and the weak supervisory section.

### 3. Copy E (Collier Emotional) — Best for Student Audiences; Supervisor Underutilised

Copy E would be the most effective copy for a student audience making an individual subscription decision. The emotional arc is well-constructed, the invisible curriculum section is the strongest statement of pedagogical philosophy in any copy, and the Dr. Nair testimonial is excellent. For my purposes as the institutional buyer, the emotional appeal is secondary to the structural case, which is underdeveloped here.

### 4. Copy C (Wiebe-Kennedy) — Good Structure; Thin on Supervision

Copy C is efficient and well-targeted to the student in crisis mode. The "not for everyone" section is admirably honest. The micro-FAQ is the most direct answer to the academic integrity question. But the institutional content is so thin it would not survive a faculty meeting, and the supervisor-facing content is essentially absent.

### 5. Copy D (Deep Work) — Right Ideas, Wrong Format

Copy D contains some of the most intellectually rigorous content in the set. The compound advantage section and the skill atrophy argument are the strongest case for Learn Mode in any copy. But the format requires 15 minutes of sustained reading that neither my students nor my colleagues will give it. The ideas should be extracted and embedded in the other copies, not left in a 4,000-word essay that few will read to the end.

## The ONE Change That Would Make Me Mandate This Tool

Across all five copies, there is one missing element that would change my decision from "conditional recommendation" to "departmental mandate":

**A dedicated supervisor-facing section that shows me the oversight dashboard in action — specifically, a before-and-after of what my supervisory workload looks like.**

Here is what that section would need to contain:

1. A specific description of what I can see in the supervisor dashboard before a student comes to me — which papers they have included, whether they ran a proper PRISMA screen, whether their PICO question maps to their search strategy, and how many revision cycles their draft has gone through in the tool.

2. A concrete example of catching a methodological error at week two versus week eight — and what that means for a supervisor who is managing eight students simultaneously while also running an OPD and teaching undergraduates.

3. A statement — specific and defensible — about what the Learn Mode interaction log shows me. Can I see that my student was actually taught by the AI rather than having text written for them? Can I see the Socratic exchange?

4. A direct answer to the question my dean will ask: "Can you demonstrate to the ethics committee that this tool cannot be used to generate text for academic submission?" Not a philosophical argument — a specific functional demonstration.

5. The removal or clear ethical framing of the "AI humanisation" feature. As it stands, this feature is the single largest obstacle to institutional recommendation. If it cannot be removed, it needs to be framed explicitly as a writing improvement tool with no application to AI detection evasion — and that framing needs to be prominent, not buried in an FAQ.

If Copy B's institutional section were expanded to include these five elements — with a specific supervisor dashboard walkthrough and a clear ethics committee answer — I would bring this to my HoD by next month.

## My Bottom Line

I came into this evaluation expecting to be disappointed. I was partially surprised.

The Learn Mode concept, as described, is the first AI tool architecture I have encountered that I could defend to an academic integrity committee without hedging. The Socratic refusal to write — the three questions instead of a paragraph — is exactly the pedagogical behaviour I want my students to experience. If that is genuinely how the product works, it is not a tool that makes students lazier. It is a tool that makes the learning process visible and structured in a way that eight simultaneous thesis supervisions cannot provide.

The methodology content — PRISMA 2020, RoB 2, Oxford CEBM evidence hierarchy, MeSH Boolean construction, triple-agent screening — is accurate and specific enough that I trust the product was built with genuine research methodology input. A tool that auto-generates a PRISMA 2020 flow diagram and tracks all 27 checklist items is not a shortcut. It is scaffolding. There is a difference.

What would make me trust this completely: seeing the product. Not the copy. The product.

What would make me block it: evidence that the humanisation feature is being used by students to evade detection. Or evidence that Learn Mode's Socratic behaviour is inconsistent — that it sometimes just writes for students when they push back.

What I would tell my students today: "I have read about a tool that claims it will teach you to write your literature review rather than writing it for you. If that claim is accurate, I would recommend it. I am going to trial it myself before I tell you anything further."

That is not endorsement. But for a deeply sceptical academic who has caught three students this year submitting AI-generated text, it is the most I am prepared to offer without seeing the product in action.

And it is more than I have offered any other AI tool.
