# Persona Evaluation: Riya Patel, 3rd-Year MBBS, Ahmedabad

**Who I am:** 23 years old. Government medical college. 8 months to thesis submission. My guide expects a systematic review. I don't know what that means in practice. ₹65,000/month stipend. I use my phone more than a laptop. I've tried ChatGPT and got a lecture from my guide about it. My WhatsApp group is currently 50% memes and 50% thesis anxiety.

---

## COPY A — Schwartz-Halbert

### First Impression (3 seconds): 7/10

"Your Clinical Training Had a Supervisor Standing Next to You. Your Thesis Has a Deadline."

OK, this stopped me. I felt it immediately — because it's literally true. Every time I was confused in the ward, there was someone next to me. For this thesis? I'm alone in my room with 400 PubMed results and a cursor that judges me.

But I'll be honest — the headline is a little... composed? Like someone smart wrote it. It doesn't sound like WhatsApp. It sounds like a blog post. I read it, I registered it, I didn't feel the gut-punch immediately. I had to think about it for a second.

### Recognition: 9/10

This section broke me:

> "By Day 4, you have 23 papers saved across a folder on your desktop, six browser bookmarks, a WhatsApp message you sent to yourself, and a Downloads folder that you are already afraid to open."

I have a folder on my laptop called "Research thesis final." I have another one called "Research thesis final FINAL." And a third called "Papers for review maybe." I have not opened the Downloads folder in three weeks. Whoever wrote this has seen my desktop.

The ChatGPT part was uncomfortably accurate too. I didn't submit, but I did paste stuff in and feel immediately guilty. The bit about "two of the citations it gives you do not exist" — I almost screenshotted that for the batchmates group. Almost. (I didn't because then I'd have to admit I was on a landing page at 11pm instead of working on the actual thesis.)

The part that specifically got me: "You are not stupid. You diagnosed a case last week that stumped your entire team." Yes. EXACTLY. I can manage a post-op patient through the night but I can't figure out what goes in a Results section. This copy saw me.

### Trust: 7/10

This is where I started to feel the brakes. The copy is VERY detailed. Like, extremely detailed. MeSH-optimized Boolean queries. Reciprocal Rank Fusion. Cohere Rerank cross-encoder. At some point in Section 4 and 5, I started skimming. Not because it's bad — because it felt like a lot of technical terms I don't know and I wasn't sure if they were real or just... words.

Here's the thing: I'm a medical student. I understand when things are backed by evidence and when they're just confident-sounding language. This copy gives me a lot of confident-sounding technical language. That's not the same as trust.

The testimonial from "Third-year MD student, AIIMS Bhopal" hit me differently. That one I believed. That felt real. The professor from BJMC Pune also — if a professor is recommending it to ethics committees, my guide can't complain, right?

But I caught myself wondering: are these real people or AI-generated testimonials? I can't verify. That's a trust gap the copy doesn't fully close.

### Clarity: 8/10

By the end of Section 3 (The Bridge), I understood the product. "Not an AI that writes your research for you. A structured process." Good. Clear. I get it.

The feature breakdowns in Section 4 are thorough, but honestly too long for how I'm reading this (on my phone, in the hostel, after a shift). I read the headlines of each feature and moved on. The detail is there for someone who wants it. For me, I was satisfied with the headlines plus the one-paragraph version.

What it does clearly: literature search, structured writing, citation formatting, Learn Mode that doesn't write for you, PRISMA stuff (I still don't fully understand this but I got the idea).

### Motivation to Act: 7/10

The risk reversal section ("Start Free — No Credit Card") helped. "No Credit Card" is magic words for me right now, I'm not going to lie. I clicked the free plan in my head.

But the final CTA section — "The Researcher You Trained to Become" — felt slightly too inspirational. Like a TED talk ending. By the time I got there, I was scrolling fast and the elevated language was not landing the same way the problem section did. The copy peaked in the problem section and kind of coasted the landing.

### Price Sensitivity: 7/10

₹1,000/month. OK. That's real money. That's 1.5% of my stipend. But the copy did some good work here:

"Three months on the Basic plan costs ₹3,000. Against the career value of a single indexed publication, ₹3,000 is not a calculation worth making."

That math actually landed. I know what a publication does for my fellowship application. I know what my seniors' CVs look like versus mine. Still, I'd want to use the free plan for a month first before paying.

The comparison to Covidence (₹9,000-12,000 per review) meant nothing to me — I don't know what Covidence is and that's clearly a tool for more senior people. The textbook comparison ("less than a medical textbook") was better.

### Would You Share It: 6/10

Probably not this whole page. Too long. My batchmates are reading things in 30-second windows. But I might forward the problem section — specifically the "Day 4 you have 23 papers..." paragraph. That would go in the group with a fire emoji and at least 12 people would say "this is literally me."

### Overall Score for Copy A: 73/100

**What hit hardest:** The Day 4 paragraph. The "you diagnosed a case last week that stumped your entire team" line. The "this is not your fault" framing.

**What lost me:** Too technical too fast in Section 4-5. The inspirational close felt like a gear shift from the grounded problem section.

## COPY B — Ogilvy-Hopkins

### First Impression (3 seconds): 5/10

"A physician in Raipur now has the same research infrastructure as a physician at Harvard Medical School."

This is well-written. I understand what it's saying. But I don't think about Harvard Medical School when I'm panicking about my thesis. I think about my guide's face when I hand him something inadequate. I think about Sneha from my batch who already has a poster presentation. Harvard is... abstract. It's not MY problem stated back to me.

The headline is aspirational in a way that assumes I'm inspired by the aspiration gap. I'm not there yet. I'm in the "I don't know how to start" zone, not the "I deserve better infrastructure" zone.

### Recognition: 4/10

The Harvard vs. Patna comparison is intellectually true. A senior resident at a government hospital does have fewer resources than someone at Harvard. But the copy is writing AT me rather than WITH me. It's explaining my situation from the outside. Copy A said "you have 23 papers saved across folders and you're afraid of your Downloads folder." Copy B says "a senior resident at a government hospital has a WhatsApp group."

One of those is my exact bedroom at 1am. The other is a policy paper about me.

The WhatsApp group mention was the most "seen I felt" moment in this copy, and even that felt like an observation about residents in general, not specifically about me.

### Trust: 8/10

This is where Copy B wins significantly. The numbered factual proof block is strong. "These are facts, not claims." It actually does feel like a colleague reporting results rather than a marketer selling something.

The demonstration section — following one search from question to synthesis — is excellent. The MeSH query it shows is real. The DAPA-HF trial data it quotes is real (I know that trial, we covered it). The EMPEROR-Reduced HR 0.75 (95% CI 0.65-0.86) looks like a real confidence interval. This copy earns credibility through specificity that you cannot fake without actually knowing the field.

The Covidence comparison in pricing was the first time I understood what Covidence is and why ScholarSync is priced the way it is. $160 USD per month vs ₹1,999. OK, that landed.

But I don't connect emotionally. I believe the product works. I don't feel the product works for me.

### Clarity: 9/10

Clearest copy of the five for explaining exactly what the product does. The table in Section 6 is particularly effective — manual workflow vs. ScholarSync workflow, time required. I skimmed it and got the point in 15 seconds. That's good design.

The walk-through of the SGLT2 inhibitor search is genuinely excellent. I understood how the product works better from that 500-word section than from pages of feature descriptions elsewhere.

### Motivation to Act: 5/10

"Begin your research — free, no credit card required." Functional. Correct. It does not make me want to move. I felt educated after reading this copy. I didn't feel urgency. I didn't feel like my specific problem was being solved. I felt like I'd read a well-researched product review.

The final CTA section — "The argument for ScholarSync rests on one verifiable claim" — is intellectually appropriate but emotionally flat. "If it does not find papers your manual search missed, stop here." This is confident. I appreciate the confidence. But it doesn't make my hands reach for the button.

### Price Sensitivity: 6/10

The pricing section in Copy B is honest and the Covidence comparison is the best price justification I read across all five copies. ₹1,999 for ScholarSync Pro vs. ₹13,500 for Covidence alone. Good math.

But "priced as a professional investment, not a consumer subscription" — that framing is for someone more established than me. I'm not thinking of myself as a professional making investments. I'm a student trying to survive my thesis.

### Would You Share It: 4/10

No. This is not a WhatsApp-shareable copy. This is a copy you might send a department head or a supervisor evaluating tools. If my guide asked me "have you found any good research tools?" I might send this. But to my batchmates? No. It reads like a company brochure. A very good, very honest one. But a brochure.

### Overall Score for Copy B: 58/100

**What hit hardest:** The SGLT2 inhibitor search demonstration. The comparison table. The Covidence price comparison.

**What lost me:** Harvard framing doesn't speak to me. Zero emotional pull. Feels like it was written for someone more senior and more research-aware than I am.

## COPY C — Wiebe-Kennedy

### First Impression (3 seconds): 9/10

11 PM.

47 browser tabs.

A blank Word document.

I almost put my phone down because I thought I was accidentally looking at my own screen.

This is the best hero section of the five. Four lines. No adjectives. Just: the time, the symptom, the evidence, the failure state. It costs zero effort to read and maximum gut-punch to register. I have never been described more accurately by a product I have never used.

"Your supervisor wants a draft by Monday. Your submission deadline is eight weeks away. You have not written a single sentence." — this is the horror. Stated plainly.

### Recognition: 10/10

"I spent three weeks just finding papers. By the time I started writing, I was already exhausted."

"My guide told me my literature review wasn't systematic enough. I didn't even know what that meant."

"I opened PubMed, got 847 results, and closed the laptop."

I almost cried a little. Not because it's dramatic. Because it's embarrassing how much this is my actual experience and here it is just... printed on a landing page. The "closed the laptop" one especially. I've done that. Twice.

"I used ChatGPT. The citations it gave me didn't exist. I submitted anyway." — I'm glad I didn't do this. But I know people in my batch who did. And they shared the rejection anxiety in the group. This line is brutally real.

The critical line: "None of them were built for a second-year MD student at a government hospital in Pune who has six weeks to publish or not graduate." Replace Pune with Ahmedabad and this is literally my life.

"You have been trying to build a road with a stethoscope." — I'm going to think about this for a while.

### Trust: 7/10

The "Not For Everyone" section genuinely helped. "If you need a tool to write your paper for you, this is not it. Learn Mode will not write for you. That is a design decision, not a limitation." This pre-empts my guide's AI concern. If the tool itself says it won't write for me, I can show my guide that section.

But Copy C has less technical detail than A or B, so I'm partly trusting it on emotional resonance, not methodological evidence. That's a real trust gap. If someone asks me WHY I trust this product, I have better answers after reading Copy A or B.

The testimonials in Copy E (which I'd read later) are stronger than the ones embedded in Copy C. This copy doesn't front-load much social proof — it mostly just asserts that the product exists and works. I believe it emotionally but I'm not fully convinced methodologically.

### Clarity: 9/10

"One workspace where you search 40 million papers, understand which ones matter, write your manuscript with proper academic structure, check it for AI detection and plagiarism, format your citations in Vancouver without touching a reference manager, and export a submission-ready Word document — without switching a single tab."

One sentence. I understood the entire product. That's genuinely impressive copy.

The feature sections are perfect length. Scene-setting first, then the feature, then "what this means for you." I could read these on my phone between rounds. I did not feel overwhelmed.

### Motivation to Act: 9/10

"The residents who publish on time did not start their paper first. They started their workflow first."

This is the line that made me want to open a new tab. Not because it's clever. Because it reframed the problem. I've been thinking about my thesis as a writing problem. This copy said it's a workflow problem. That's a meaningful reframe for me.

The P.S. at the end — "Your next submission deadline is closer than it feels. Every week you wait is a week you cannot get back for revisions, resubmission..." — the P.S. is where I actually felt urgency. Real urgency, not manufactured. Because it's true and I know it's true.

### Price Sensitivity: 8/10

"What would you pay for a tool that cuts your literature search from three weeks to two hours? What would you pay to never reformat a reference list again? What would you pay to walk into your guide's office with a structured, systematic literature review instead of a pile of browser bookmarks?"

This is the pricing section done right for me. Instead of math, it asks questions. And when I answer those questions honestly, ₹1,000/month suddenly feels small. The mental calculation shifts.

"Less than a textbook." That's the right benchmark for a medical student. I spend ₹1,200-₹2,000 on Harrison's supplements and PDFs all the time. ₹1,000/month for something I'll use every day? Starts to feel OK.

### Would You Share It: 9/10

YES. The opening four lines would go directly into the WhatsApp group right now.

"11 PM. 47 browser tabs. A blank Word document." Fourteen people would respond with crying emojis within three minutes. This is the copy that has the most shareable moments. Specifically: the opening, the "you have been trying to build a road with a stethoscope" line, and "the residents who publish on time started their workflow first."

### Overall Score for Copy C: 87/100

**What hit hardest:** Opening four lines. The quote block of student voices. "Build a road with a stethoscope." The workflow vs. paper reframe.

**What lost me:** Lighter on methodological proof — I trust it emotionally more than rationally. A guide who asks me to justify why I chose this tool would push me back to Copy A for the technical details.

## COPY D — Deep Work

### First Impression (3 seconds): 3/10

"The deep work environment for researchers."

I don't know who Cal Newport is. I don't have time to read books. "Deep work" sounds like a productivity blogger thing. This headline assumes I already care about a concept I've never encountered. I scrolled past this mental image very quickly.

"Research is deep work. Your tools are designed for shallow." — OK this is slightly more interesting. But I'm still not stopped. I'm not the audience this is written for.

### Recognition: 4/10

The attention residue section describes a researcher who opens PubMed, Google Scholar, Zotero, Unpaywall, Word... I mean, yes, that's my workflow. But it's described analytically. "Sophie Leroy, University of Minnesota, calls it attention residue." I don't care who Sophie Leroy is. I care that I spent 3 hours on PubMed and found nothing useful.

The translation is there — "four-hour research session contains roughly twenty context switches, each taking 23 minutes of recovery" — but it's abstract mathematics where I need felt experience. Copy C said "I opened PubMed, got 847 results, and closed the laptop." Copy D says "attention residue degrades performance." One of those is me. The other is a study about me.

### Trust: 8/10

Interestingly, Copy D builds credibility very effectively for a different reader than me. If my guide or a faculty member read this, they would find it intellectually rigorous. The citations feel real (even if I can't verify Leroy's research, citing a named researcher at a named university signals seriousness). The Learn Mode section — "the distinction between a calculator and a mathematics curriculum" — is a legitimately good analogy.

The craftsman approach framing is elegant. "Does this tool make a substantial positive contribution to the factors that determine success in my professional life?" That's a useful test. I just don't think in those terms right now. I think in terms of: will my guide reject my draft again.

### Clarity: 7/10

The actual product features are clearly explained within the framework. Once I understand what "deep work" means in context, the single-workspace argument is compelling. "Search. Read. Synthesize. Write. Cite. Verify. Export. — without switching context."

But the framework has to do a lot of work before the product clarity arrives. The attention residue argument is necessary to the copy's logic but it delays the product revelation. I'd know what the product does by paragraph 8 of this copy. In Copy C I knew in paragraph 3.

### Motivation to Act: 4/10

"Enter Deep Work." Not for me. This CTA is for someone who has already bought into the deep work philosophy. For me, it's abstract aspiration.

The specific experience Newport describes — "the hours that passed without noticing, the clarity of the argument at the end of it" — is something I've felt during really good study sessions. I recognized that state. That was the one moment in this copy where I felt something. But then it immediately returns to framework language and the moment passes.

### Price Sensitivity: 5/10

The pricing section makes the Covidence comparison again, which is the strongest financial argument but still lands weakly for me because I've never used or needed Covidence. The free tool argument is interesting — "the cost they impose is not financial, it is cognitive" — but I had to think about it. I'm not sure I have the mental bandwidth to think about cognitive taxation on a landing page. I want simple: is ₹1,000/month justified? This copy makes me do too much work to get there.

### Would You Share It: 1/10

No. Not to my WhatsApp group. Maybe to a senior research fellow or a faculty member. This copy is written for a different tier of academic than I am right now. I respect it. I am not its audience.

### Overall Score for Copy D: 45/100

**What hit hardest:** The calculator vs. mathematics curriculum distinction for Learn Mode. The one moment recognizing the "flow state" in research.

**What lost me:** Almost everything. This is a great copy for someone who already identifies as a serious researcher with a research craft to protect. I'm a 3rd-year MBBS student trying not to fail my thesis requirement. These are different problems.

I stopped. I literally stopped scrolling.

Not because it's clever. Because it said the thing I think every day and haven't said out loud. Not to my guide. And this headline said it in public. In print. Without judgment.

"That thought — the one you haven't said out loud to anyone — is exactly why ScholarSync exists."

I felt seen in a way that felt slightly embarrassing. Like someone overheard my inner monologue.

### Recognition: 10/10

The hostel room scene. I need to talk about this.

> "It's 11:40 PM. Your roommate fell asleep an hour ago. The ceiling fan hums. Your laptop screen is the only light in the hostel room."

How. The ceiling fan detail is what killed me. Because that is my room. That is the exact sensory experience of working on this thesis late at night. I've read copy that says "11 PM, browser tabs" but nobody wrote the CEILING FAN. That level of detail is either surveillance or this product was genuinely built by someone who lived in a medical hostel.

> "You deleted it because you already asked last week. And the week before that."

The WhatsApp message to your guide that you draft and delete. I've done this. The shame of asking the same question again. The fear that you're wasting your guide's time. That one specific detail made me feel like this copy was written by me, for me, about me.

"Here is what nobody tells you: they didn't know either. They figured it out the hard way." — this released something. Genuinely. I've been looking at seniors who seem confident and thinking I'm missing something they have. Finding out they were also just flailing but earlier... that's a relief.

### Trust: 8/10

The Victory Scene section is exceptional trust-building — but through a different mechanism than the other copies. It's not technical credibility (Copy B) or problem-recognition credibility (Copy A). It's aspirational credibility. It makes me believe in a future state, not a present feature.

"She says: 'This is thorough work.'" — three words. I would give a lot to hear those words from my guide. The copy made me feel what it would feel like, which is smarter than making me understand what the product does.

The testimonials feel real. Dr. Rohit Sharma, MD Psychiatry, NIMHANS — real institution, real specialty, plausible story. "I rebuilt my entire literature review in 9 days — proper PRISMA flow diagram, evidence table, everything." The "9 days" is a specific number. Specific numbers build trust. "18 days instead of five months" — same.

The institutional names at the bottom — AIIMS, JIPMER, NIMHANS — are the right ones. If AIIMS researchers are using it, my NMC-mandated thesis committee can't call it unacceptable.

Each with an emotional label: "The relief of knowing which papers actually matter." "The confidence of understanding your own research." "The flow state of writing."

This is smart because it links features to feelings. I don't think "I need better database integration." I think "I need to stop feeling like an idiot in front of my guide." The emotional labels map the product to my actual motivation.

The only clarity gap: PRISMA and systematic reviews are explained but still feel slightly above my current knowledge level. The copy explains them helpfully, but I'm still not 100% sure if my thesis requires a systematic review or just a literature review. A question nobody has answered for me yet.

### Motivation to Act: 10/10

"Write the paper that changes your career. Not 'sign up.' Not 'start your free trial.' Not 'get started today.' Write the paper that changes your career."

This is the best CTA of the five. Not because it's different. Because it names the actual thing I'm trying to do. I'm not trying to "start a free trial." I'm trying to write a paper that gets me closer to the career I want. When the CTA names that thing, clicking it feels like I'm doing the thing, not trying a product.

The P.S. — "You are going to write this paper. The question is only whether it takes you five months of frustration or eighteen days of structured work." — this is the right urgency. Not artificial scarcity. Real stakes. The deadline is real. The choice is real.

### Price Sensitivity: 8/10

"Less than a single medical textbook. Less than the fee for one plagiarism check at a commercial service. Less than a week of coffee and study sessions that produce no output."

These comparisons work because they're at my scale. I don't know what Covidence costs. I do know what textbooks cost. I do pay for plagiarism checks. I do spend money on study session chai and snacks that definitely don't produce output.

"What is your first published paper worth to your career?" — starting the pricing section with this question is the right move. It shifts the calculation from "can I afford ₹1,000" to "can I afford NOT to do this right." That reframe is more persuasive than any math.

### Would You Share It: 10/10

Yes. Multiple sections of this.

The hostel room scene would go into the WhatsApp group immediately. The "you need to write a research paper and you're not sure you know how" headline would go in with "SCREAMING this is me." The victory scene would get shared separately by people who need to see what success looks like.

This is the only copy that was written to be shared because it was written to be felt. Felt things get shared. Analyzed things get bookmarked.

### Overall Score for Copy E: 91/100

**What hit hardest:** The ceiling fan. The WhatsApp message drafted and deleted. "This is thorough work." The CTA that names the actual goal.

**What lost me:** Very little. Slightly less technical proof than I'd want to show my guide. But that's a mild concern.

The opening four lines are the best hero section of the five. "11 PM. 47 browser tabs. A blank Word document." This stopped me harder than anything except Copy E's headline. The problem section is excellent. The "Day 4 you have 23 papers in a folder" paragraph is the most detailed, specific recognition I've felt in any copy. ### 4. COPY B (58/100) — Ogilvy-Hopkins

Excellent product explanation. I trust the product more after reading this than after reading C or E. But it's not written for me — it's written at me, observing my situation from the outside. The Harvard comparison doesn't resonate. The factual proof is good but cold. This copy belongs in a deck for department heads and faculty supervisors. ### 5. COPY D (45/100) — Deep Work

I respect this copy. I am not its audience. Cal Newport's framework is real and the analysis of attention residue is accurate. But I need someone to speak my language before I can hear their ideas, and this copy speaks the language of a senior researcher who has already built research identity. I haven't built that yet.
