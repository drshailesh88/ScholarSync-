# Persona Evaluation: Dr. Arjun Mehta
## Senior Resident, Internal Medicine, AIIMS Delhi | Age 28
## Task: Systematic Review — SGLT2 Inhibitors in Heart Failure

---

**Context for this evaluation:**

I am leading a systematic review on SGLT2 inhibitors in heart failure for a high-impact Indian journal. My department head assigned me this. I have published two case reports. I have never done a proper systematic review. I know PRISMA exists — I have seen it cited in papers — but I have never implemented it. I have 60+ hours of clinical duties per week. I earn ₹85,000/month. My real constraints are time and methodological credibility. I cannot afford to submit a paper that gets torn apart in peer review for methodology failures. I also cannot afford to be seen as someone who used AI to write their paper. I need a tool that makes my work faster AND more defensible, not one that does the work for me and leaves me exposed.

I evaluated all five copies on a Tuesday evening between post-rounds notes and a department meeting. I had about 40 minutes. Here is my honest reaction to each.

## COPY A — Schwartz-Halbert Framework

### First Impression: 6/10

The headline — *"Your Clinical Training Had a Supervisor Standing Next to You. Your Thesis Has a Deadline."* — is clever. I noticed it. But it aims at a PG student in their first year, not a senior resident who already knows the deadline pressure and already has two papers under their belt. The problem agitation section — the one about 400 PubMed results, the blank Word document, the ChatGPT citations that don't exist — is vivid and accurate. I have been in that room. But I was in that room three years ago. Today my problem is not "how do I start" — my problem is "how do I run a methodology my department head and a peer reviewer cannot attack."

The hero section would stop a second-year resident in their tracks. For me, it's a half-stop. I read on, but slightly bored.

### Professional Credibility: 7/10

This copy gets significantly better once it moves past the agitation into features. The Feature 1 section on Deep Research is strong — MeSH-optimised Boolean, triple-database, evidence level badges. When it says "You will never again accidentally treat a case report as equivalent evidence to an RCT," that lands for me. That is a real mistake that appears in real papers from our institution.

The PRISMA feature section (Feature 4) is the best part of this copy. It describes triple-agent AI screening with conflict resolution, PRISMA 2020 flow diagram auto-generation, RoB 2 domain-level assessment. This is the language I needed to hear earlier. If this section appeared in the first half of the copy, I would be leaning forward from minute one instead of minute ten.

The Learn Mode section is thoughtful and the ethics argument is genuine, but it reads like it was written for someone who needs to be convinced not to cheat. I am not that person. I want to be told this tool makes rigorous methodology easier to execute, not that it prevents me from taking shortcuts.

### Time-to-Value: 7/10

"Most residents spend 12-15 hours on literature search alone. ScholarSync users complete this in under two hours." — This is the kind of specific, auditable claim I respond to. I want to verify it, which means it landed. The comparison table in the systematic review feature is excellent: manually building a PRISMA flow diagram takes 2-4 hours; this generates it in real time. I believe it.

The math section is honest and reasonably compelling. Covidence at USD 119 per review (roughly ₹10,000-12,000) versus ₹2,000/month for Pro with the full systematic review suite — that comparison does the work it needs to do for me specifically, since I was already thinking about whether I needed Covidence.

### Defensibility: 7/10

The copy explicitly addresses the "can I cite this in my Methods section" question for triple-agent screening ("methodologically equivalent to dual-reviewer independent screening with consensus resolution — the Cochrane-recommended approach"). That is exactly the sentence I needed to see. The RoB 2 automation across all five Cochrane domains — randomisation, deviations from intervention, missing outcome data, measurement of outcome, selection of reported results — is specific enough to feel real.

What is missing: I want to know what I write in my Methods section. Not just that it is defensible — tell me the sentence I can actually write. Copy B does this better.

### Technical Depth: 7/10

PRISMA, RoB 2, Cochrane methodology, MeSH Boolean, Reciprocal Rank Fusion, Cohere Rerank, HyDE. These are real terms, used correctly. Whoever wrote this copy did the homework. The evidence hierarchy explanation (Oxford CEBM, Level I through Level V) is accurate and clinically relevant. I did not catch a single methodological error in this section, which is a non-trivial test to pass.

The FAQ on PRISMA compliance (FAQ 5) is the best FAQ I have seen in any software landing page in the medical space. It answers the actual question: what does PRISMA 2020 compliance mean in practice, and what specifically does ScholarSync produce? The answer is concrete: the 27-item checklist tracks in real time, the SVG flow diagram updates as you work, the search strategy is output formatted for direct paste into your Methods section. This is publishable-methodology language.

### Motivation to Act: 6/10

The identity close — "The Researcher You Trained to Become" — is genuinely good writing. But it falls slightly flat for me because the rest of the copy worked so hard to speak to an earlier-stage researcher that the identity pivot at the end feels like it is addressing someone who is not quite me. I am not trying to become a researcher. I already am one. I am trying to run a rigorous systematic review under severe time pressure without embarrassing my department.

The free plan offer is clean and believable. No credit card, no expiry. The refund policy is stated simply. These things matter.

### Price Sensitivity: 7/10

At ₹2,000/month for Pro — the tier I would actually need for the systematic review suite — this is 2.4% of my monthly income. For one month of a systematic review, that is a serious but not unreasonable number. The Covidence comparison makes it feel rational. The framing of "three months on Basic at ₹3,000 versus the career value of one indexed publication" is the right frame, but the copy overworks it slightly. I can do that math myself. Just tell me the Covidence comparison and move on.

### Overall Score: 6.7/10

**Honest reaction:** This is a well-built, methodologically serious copy that would convert PG students in their first or second year at high rates. For me, as a senior resident running a real systematic review, it reads about 30% too junior in its framing and 70% exactly right in its technical depth. If I encountered this copy, I would save the PRISMA FAQ to read again later and probably sign up for the free plan to test it. I would not subscribe on the basis of this copy alone.

I am not in Raipur. I am at AIIMS Delhi. But the access parity argument applies to me in a different way: I do not have a Harvard medical librarian. I do not have Covidence paid for by my department. I do not have a research methodology supervisor with office hours. I have PubMed, my clinical instincts, and whatever methodology I can absorb between cases. The argument is accurate for me even though the geographic framing was aimed somewhere else.

This copy treats me like a professional evaluating a tool, not a student needing to be reassured. That tonal decision is exactly right for where I am.

### Professional Credibility: 9/10

The Factual Proof Block (Section 2) is the best single section in all five copies for a reader like me. Ten numbered, factual claims, stated without hedging:

1. Searches three databases simultaneously, results in under 30 seconds.
2. Every result classified by evidence level, Level I through Level V.
3. MeSH-optimized Boolean generated automatically — "constructing this query correctly by hand requires training that most medical librarians take years to develop."
4. Reciprocal Rank Fusion + Cohere Rerank — specific, auditable ranking methodology.
5. Cochrane-methodology search strategy from PICO input — formatted with field tags, explosion terms, subheadings.
6. Triple-agent screening — citable in a systematic review's Methods section.
7. PRISMA 2020 flow diagram in real time, SVG export, 27-item checklist.
8. RoB 2 automated per paper, domain-level judgments, auditable.
9. ProseMirror editor with 20 academic-specific extensions. IMRAD, CARE, STROBE as contextual guidance.
10. Export formatted to journal submission standards. No manual reformatting.

This is how you speak to someone who is trained in evidence evaluation. Give me your evidence. Let me judge it. Do not try to make me feel something before you have earned it.

The phrase "the triple-agent AI screening system... is citable in a systematic review's Methods section" is the single most important sentence across all five copies. That is the sentence I have been looking for. It answers my central question: can I write this in my methods and defend it?

### Time-to-Value: 8/10

The demonstration in Section 4 is Hopkins at his best. A senior resident at NIMS Hyderabad, searching "What is the evidence for SGLT2 inhibitors in heart failure with reduced ejection fraction?" — this is almost exactly my paper. Reading this section, I felt a strong physical sensation of recognition. The actual PubMed query it shows:

`("Sodium-Glucose Transporter 2 Inhibitors"[MeSH Terms] OR "SGLT2 inhibitor*"[tiab]) AND ("Heart Failure"[MeSH Terms] AND "reduced ejection fraction"[tiab]) AND ("2015"[PDat]:"2026"[PDat])`

That is a real, correctly formatted MeSH Boolean query. I have seen medical librarian-constructed queries in my institution and this looks right. The EMPEROR-Reduced and DAPA-HF trials appear at the top. The 2023 Cochrane review with I² = 14% is cited correctly. The fact that the copy bothers to get the actual trials and statistics right — rather than inventing plausible-sounding examples — tells me this was built by someone who actually knows the SGLT2 literature.

22 minutes for a complete search strategy. I believe that number, because the demonstration showed me the mechanism behind it.

### Defensibility: 9/10

Section 5 — "Built to the standard your journal reviewer expects" — is where this copy earns its highest marks from me. The language is exactly right:

"A medical information specialist reviewing your search strategy will find it constructed correctly." Not "your guide will be impressed." A medical information specialist will find it correct. This is the specific person I am worried about — not my department head, who trusts me, but the peer reviewer who is a methodology expert and will look for weaknesses.

"This is auditable. It can be included in a systematic review's supplementary materials." — The RoB 2 section says this explicitly. Auditable is a word I use when I think about defensibility. Finding it here, used precisely, signals to me that this copy was written by someone who understands what peer review actually scrutinizes.

"In a manuscript's Methods section, you can state: 'Evidence was graded according to the Oxford CEBM hierarchy.'" — This is giving me the actual sentence. The copy is doing my work for me in the best possible way: not by writing my methods, but by showing me exactly what I can claim.

### Technical Depth: 9/10

This is the only copy that uses "HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embeddings)" and explains what it does and why it matters for research retrieval. It is the only copy that mentions EMTREE terms alongside MeSH as the EMBASE equivalent. It knows that PubMed uses field tags like [tiab] and [MeSH Terms] and [Subheading]. It knows that "explosion terms" are a specific PubMed feature. It knows that Vancouver is the ICMJE standard, not just "a medical citation format."

The description of RoB 2 correctly names all five domains in the right order and correctly distinguishes domain-level from overall risk judgment. The description of PRISMA 2020 correctly identifies 27 checklist items (not 25, not 30 — 27).

For a senior resident trying to evaluate whether a tool genuinely understands the methodology or is performing it, this level of specificity is the test. This copy passes.

### Motivation to Act: 8/10

The CTA section is unusual and strong: "Bring your actual research question. Run it through ScholarSync on the Free plan. Compare the result to what your current workflow produces in the same time. If ScholarSync does not find papers your manual search missed... stop here. The product has not earned your subscription."

I respect this enormously. The tool is so confident in its performance that it is willing to be tested head-to-head against my existing workflow before I spend anything. That is not marketing language. That is the language of someone who has already won the comparison and knows it.

The institutional section — written for department heads and research supervisors — is a nice touch. I am not a department head, but I am thinking about the fact that my department head assigned me this review. The framing of "catching a methodological error in week two versus week eight" is exactly the kind of argument I could use to recommend this tool to my supervisor.

### Price Sensitivity: 8/10

The Covidence comparison is the most precise and honest pricing argument in all five copies: "Covidence costs approximately $160 USD per month for individual researchers — roughly ₹13,500. ScholarSync Pro costs ₹1,999 and includes capabilities Covidence does not have: integrated writing, citation management, evidence-level classification, and a complete writing editor."

I was planning to look into Covidence. This tells me not to bother. ₹1,999/month for Pro versus ₹13,500/month for Covidence alone — with ScholarSync including more features — is a decision that requires no further deliberation.

The pricing framing as "a professional investment, not a consumer subscription" is precisely the right register for a senior resident who is making a career-level decision, not buying an app.

### Overall Score: 8.2/10

**Honest reaction:** This is the copy I would show to my department head to justify the subscription expense. It speaks to me as a professional, not as a student. The SGLT2 demonstration in Section 4 almost made me stop reading and open the tool immediately. The factual proof block is the most efficient use of page space I have seen on a landing page for any research tool. This copy would convert me.

Consensus algorithm: unanimous results proceed automatically; any disagreement triggers human review. That last sentence is the one. Not just what it does but why it was built that way — because the people building it knew systematic review methodology from the inside. That is the signal I am looking for.

"A four-hour research session, interrupted by tool-switching every eleven to twelve minutes, contains roughly twenty context switches. Each switch takes 23 minutes of recovery time to fully absorb." The compound advantage section (Section 7) — five years of deep research practice versus five years of AI-assisted research — is genuinely compelling as a career argument. If Copy D means $148/year, that is wrong. If it means $148/month, the "per year" qualifier is wrong. The CTA — "Enter Deep Work" — is philosophically consistent with the copy's tone but practically weak. It does not tell me what specifically happens when I click the button. ### Overall Score: 6.8/10

**Honest reaction:** This is the copy I would read if I were a post-doctoral researcher with a stable income, established research habits, and the luxury of thinking about my intellectual trajectory over the next five years.
