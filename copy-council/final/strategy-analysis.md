## 1. The Copy Council Verdict

### The Winner: Copy E (Collier Emotional Arc) for Students, Copy B (Ogilvy-Hopkins) for Everyone Else

The council did not produce a single winner. It produced a split decision that reveals the fundamental tension in ScholarSync's market.

**The voting pattern:**

| Persona | #1 Pick | #2 Pick | Core Driver |
|---|---|---|---|
| Riya (3rd-year MBBS) | Copy E (91) | Copy C (87) | Emotional recognition |
| Arjun (Senior Resident) | Copy B (8.2) | Copy E (7.4) | Methodological defensibility |
| Priya (Professor) | Copy B | Copy A | Institutional defensibility |
| Vikram (Post-doc) | Copy B (Rank 1) | Copy D (Rank 2) | Technical accuracy |
| Sunita (Dept Head) | Copy B | Copy E | ROI and scalability |
| Ananya (VC) | Copy A | Copy D | Founder signal and retention |
| Rajesh (Acquirer) | Copy B | Copy E | Technical depth and India penetration |

**Copy B wins 4 of 7 first-place votes.** Copy E wins 1 first-place but appears in 3 second-place slots. Copy A wins 1 first-place (the VC, notably). Copy C wins zero first-place votes but scores 87/100 from the primary buyer persona.

### What the Pattern Reveals

The council exposed a **two-tier market structure**:

**Tier 1 -- Emotional Buyers (students, junior residents):** They buy because they feel understood. Copy E and Copy C dominate here. The ceiling fan detail, the WhatsApp message drafted and deleted, the "you're not sure you know how" admission -- these are conversion drivers for individuals making personal subscription decisions on their phones at 11 PM.

**Tier 2 -- Rational Buyers (senior residents, professors, department heads, investors, acquirers):** They buy because they can defend the decision. Copy B dominates here. The SGLT2 demonstration, the factual proof block, the "citable in a Methods section" language, the institutional dashboard -- these are procurement drivers for people who must justify the spend to someone else.

**The tension:** Student-optimized copy (E, C) converts the individual user who seeds institutional adoption. Institution-optimized copy (B) closes the department deal that generates ARR. You need both. The question is sequencing.

### The Surprise Result

Copy D (Deep Work) ranked last or near-last for every user persona but ranked #2 for the VC. The VC saw what the users could not: the deep work philosophy is not a conversion tool -- it is a retention mechanism and a brand moat. The compound advantage argument, the five-year trajectory comparison, and the "environment for a career, not a tool for the next paper" positioning are what separate ScholarSync from every AI wrapper in the market. Copy D is not landing page copy. It is the Series A narrative.

## 2. The One Positioning That Works for Everyone

### The Unified Positioning Statement

> **ScholarSync is the structured research environment that takes medical professionals from research question to submitted manuscript -- teaching them the methodology along the way, not writing for them.**

### Why This Works Across All Personas

- **For Riya (student):** "Takes me from question to manuscript" solves the blank-page paralysis. "Teaching me" removes the shame of not knowing.
- **For Arjun (senior resident):** "Structured research environment" signals methodological rigor. "Submitted manuscript" is the outcome he needs.
- **For Priya (professor):** "Teaching them, not writing for them" is the ethics committee sentence. This is what she has been looking for.
- **For Sunita (dept head):** "Structured" means standardizable across 30 students. "Research question to submitted manuscript" is the measurable outcome.
- **For Ananya (VC):** "Environment" signals retention (not a one-time tool). "Teaching" signals Learn Mode as behavioral lock-in. The full workflow signals TAM expansion.
- **For Rajesh (acquirer):** "Medical professionals" defines the niche. "Research question to submitted manuscript" defines the product scope. The integration potential is visible.

### The Tagline

> **Write the paper. Build the skill. Own the methodology.**

This tagline works because it names three things in ascending order of defensibility: the immediate outcome (paper), the medium-term value (skill), and the long-term moat (methodology ownership). Students hear "write the paper." Professors hear "build the skill." VCs hear "own the methodology."

### The One-Liner

> **The end-to-end research workspace for Indian medical professionals -- search, synthesize, write, and publish, with an AI mentor that teaches you the methodology instead of doing it for you.**

### The Elevator Pitch (30 seconds)

> "Every year, 75,000 Indian medical postgraduates are required to publish research to graduate. Almost none of them have been taught how. They cobble together PubMed, Word, Zotero, and ChatGPT -- and produce work their supervisors send back. ScholarSync replaces that entire fragmented workflow with one workspace: literature search across 40 million papers with evidence-level classification, a PRISMA-compliant systematic review engine, a writing editor built for academic manuscripts, and a Socratic AI mentor that teaches research methodology instead of writing for students. We are the only AI research tool a university ethics committee can recommend without reservation. We charge a thousand rupees a month. Covidence alone costs ten times that."

## 3. The Messaging Hierarchy

### Hero (Above the Fold)

**Lead with the problem, not the product.**

The council was unanimous on this: every persona responded most strongly to copy that named their exact situation before naming the solution. The hero must pass the "three-second A-pile test" -- a medical resident scrolling on their phone at 11 PM must think "this is exactly what I'm dealing with" before they read a second line.

**Recommended hero:**

> **11 PM. 47 browser tabs. A blank Word document.**
> Your supervisor wants a draft by Monday. Your submission deadline is eight weeks away.
> You have not written a single sentence.
>
> *ScholarSync is how 4,000+ Indian medical professionals got from here to "submitted."*

**Why this hero:** Copy C's opening four lines scored 9/10 from Riya (the primary buyer). Copy E's "you're not sure you know how" scored 10/10 but is riskier -- it names shame directly, which some readers may resist. The 11 PM / browser tabs / blank document approach is universally recognized and carries zero risk of alienating any segment.

### Middle (Proof Section)

**Prove three things, in this order:**

1. **The product understands medical research methodology.** Show the MeSH Boolean query (from Copy B). Show the PRISMA flow diagram. Show the RoB 2 domain assessment. Vikram, Arjun, and Priya all said the same thing: "Show me you know this field. Don't tell me -- show me." The SGLT2 demonstration from Copy B was called "the single most credible technical moment" by two separate personas.

2. **The product teaches, not writes.** Learn Mode is the institutional unlock. Every professor and department head said the same thing: "If I can tell my ethics committee that this tool refuses to write for students, I can recommend it." The three laws of Learn Mode must appear prominently. The Socratic interaction must be demonstrated, not described.

3. **The product is built for India.** NMC awareness. Vancouver as default citation format. IJCM/JAPI/IJMR as named target journals. Indian rupee pricing via Razorpay. The hostel room. The guide-student dynamic. The acquirer (Rajesh) called this "the moat that matters most" -- cultural calibration that cannot be bought from a Western engineering team.

### Close (CTA)

**The CTA that won the council:**

> **Write the paper that changes your career.**

Copy E's CTA scored 10/10 from Riya and 9/10 from Arjun. It was called "the best CTA across all five copies" by two personas independently. It works because it names the actual thing the user is trying to do -- not "start a free trial" but "write the paper." Clicking the button feels like beginning the work, not beginning a subscription.

**Secondary CTA for institutional visitors:**

> **See what your department's research output looks like with ScholarSync.** [Contact for Institutional Pricing]

### Flow Architecture for Different Reader Types

**Scanners (70% of traffic -- mobile, between shifts, hostel wifi):**
- Hero section (problem recognition) -- 3 seconds
- Social proof strip (AIIMS, JIPMER, NIMHANS logos) -- 2 seconds
- Three feature cards with emotional labels ("The relief of knowing which papers matter") -- 10 seconds
- Pricing with "less than a textbook" anchor -- 5 seconds
- CTA: "Write the paper that changes your career" -- 2 seconds
- Total decision time: ~22 seconds

**Deep readers (30% of traffic -- senior residents, faculty, evaluating for institutional purchase):**
- Hero section (problem recognition)
- Full methodology section (SGLT2 demo, MeSH query, PRISMA walkthrough)
- Learn Mode three-law explanation with Socratic interaction example
- Comparison table (ScholarSync vs. manual workflow, with time per task)
- Covidence pricing comparison (accurate numbers only)
- Institutional section with supervisor dashboard description
- FAQ with PRISMA compliance, AI detection, and ethics committee answers
- CTA with institutional pricing contact
- Total evaluation time: ~12 minutes

## 4. The Moat Map

### What the Personas Collectively Revealed About Defensibility

The seven personas, evaluated independently, converged on four distinct moats -- each visible to a different stakeholder tier.

### Moat 1: Learn Mode as Distribution Mechanism (VC Insight)

Ananya (VC) identified Learn Mode as the single strongest signal of market depth across all five copies. Her reasoning:

> "Every AI tool in the academic research space is facing the same academic integrity problem. Universities are banning them. Ethics committees are flagging them. Faculty are telling students not to use them. A founder who builds the refusal to write into the product architecture -- not a policy statement, not a terms-of-service clause, but product design -- has solved a problem competitors have not addressed."

**The distribution consequence:** ScholarSync is the only AI research tool an Indian university ethics committee can recommend without reservation. Institutional word-of-mouth in Indian medical education travels through faculty recommendations and ethics committee approvals. Learn Mode converts institutional skeptics into institutional advocates. This is not a feature. It is a distribution channel.

**Competitive implication:** Elicit, SciSpace, Consensus, and Jenni AI cannot replicate this without fundamentally redesigning their products. Their core value proposition is "AI does the work for you." ScholarSync's core value proposition is "AI teaches you to do the work." These are architecturally incompatible positions. Switching from one to the other requires a product philosophy change, not a feature addition.

### Moat 2: 71-Table Schema as Engineering Depth (Acquirer Insight)

Rajesh (acquirer) identified the 71-table database schema disclosure as the strongest technical diligence signal. His reasoning:

> "A 71-table schema in a research management application suggests relational complexity -- paper metadata, library collections, user documents, citations, screening decisions, PRISMA checklist states, RoB assessments, synthesis outputs, version history, citation graph traversal results. That is not a weekend project. That is not a wrapper around an API. That is a real product with real data architecture."

**Build vs. buy assessment:** Rajesh's team estimated 18 months and approximately $3.2M in engineering cost to build equivalent functionality internally -- and that estimate does not account for the ethnographic user research, the India regulatory calibration, or the word-of-mouth distribution already embedded in the AIIMS/JIPMER/NIMHANS network.

**Additional technical moat signals across copies:** ProseMirror with 20 academic-specific extensions (months of domain-encoded engineering), HyDE retrieval (non-trivial and correctly named), Reciprocal Rank Fusion + Cohere Rerank v3.5 (coherent retrieval pipeline), dual-model Claude architecture (inference cost optimization that signals real usage).

### Moat 3: India Cultural Specificity as Geographic Moat

Every persona noted the India-specific calibration. The acquirer called it "the moat that matters most for our India strategy, because we cannot buy it from a technology team that has never done the ethnographic work." Specific signals:

- **NMC awareness:** Regulatory alignment with the body that sets thesis requirements for all Indian medical postgraduates
- **Vancouver as default:** The citation format standard for medical journals, chosen as default over APA -- a decision that only makes sense if you built for medical researchers from day one
- **Named Indian journals:** IJCM, JAPI, IJMR as target journals in the AI context
- **Hindi-English mixing:** The system handles mixed-language input naturally
- **Hostel room ethnography:** The ceiling fan, the WhatsApp message to your guide, the draft-and-delete shame cycle -- details that only emerge from genuine proximity to the user
- **Rupee pricing via Razorpay:** Not USD converted to INR, but INR-native pricing calibrated to stipend economics

**Why this is a moat, not just positioning:** No Western-built research tool has this calibration. Building it requires either hiring from within the Indian medical education system or conducting extensive ethnographic research that takes 12-18 months. By the time Elicit or SciSpace decides to enter this market, ScholarSync's institutional distribution network (seeded through faculty recommendations at AIIMS, JIPMER, NIMHANS) will have created switching costs that are social, not technical.

### Moat 4: PRISMA Workflow as Methodology Lock-in

The PRISMA systematic review suite was identified by every technical persona (Arjun, Vikram, Priya) as the feature most likely to create switching costs. The reason: a systematic review conducted in ScholarSync accumulates state -- screening decisions, PRISMA flow diagram data, RoB 2 assessments, data extraction tables, search strategy documentation. Migrating this state to another tool mid-review is prohibitively costly.

Vikram noted that Covidence's key advantage is not features but audit trail -- an established, auditable record that peer reviewers accept. If ScholarSync can establish the same auditability standard, the switching cost becomes methodological, not just practical: a researcher who has cited "ScholarSync triple-agent screening" in their Methods section has created a precedent they will maintain for future reviews.

## 5. Critical Warnings

### Warning 1: The AI Humanisation Risk (Prof. Priya's Flag)

Professor Priya Sharma identified the single most dangerous feature contradiction in the product:

> "The copy cannot simultaneously claim the moral high ground on academic integrity and offer a feature designed to help students evade detection tools. That contradiction, if a colleague raises it in a department meeting, kills the institutional recommendation."

The Pro plan includes "AI humanisation -- light, medium, heavy." In the context of a product whose entire institutional sales pitch is "we teach, we don't write," this feature is a liability. The product brief frames humanisation as "making your writing more distinctly yours." Professor Priya did not buy that framing.

**Recommendation:** Either remove humanisation from the landing page copy entirely, reframe it explicitly and prominently as a writing improvement tool with a clear ethical boundary statement, or move it to a separate "Writing Polish" feature name that does not carry the connotation of detection evasion. This must be resolved before any institutional sales conversation. It is the single largest obstacle to department-level adoption.

### Warning 2: The Factual Accuracy Imperative (Copy C's Covidence Error)

Copy C cited Covidence pricing at "$3,500 per review." Vikram (post-doc who pays for Covidence) identified this as factually wrong by one to two orders of magnitude. His response was categorical:

> "Any copy that gets this fact wrong cannot be trusted on methodology claims."

Dr. Sunita (dept head) independently flagged the same error: "Getting a competitor's pricing wrong in your copy signals either carelessness or deliberate misrepresentation. Neither builds trust with a procurement decision-maker."

The Covidence pricing varies across copies: Copy B says $160/month, Copy D says $148/year, Copy C says $3,500/review. The actual pricing is approximately $49/month for individual unlimited plans and higher for institutional configurations. This inconsistency across copies damages credibility with the exact audience segment (post-docs and faculty evaluating a Covidence replacement) that represents the highest-LTV conversion.

**Recommendation:** Standardize the Covidence comparison to a single, verified, defensible figure. Use the individual researcher rate ($49/month or equivalent annual) as the comparison baseline, not the institutional rate. State the comparison with the same precision the product applies to research methodology. If the pricing cannot be verified to the dollar, use a range with a citation.

### Warning 3: The Retention Question (VC's Concern About Post-Thesis Churn)

Ananya (VC) named this as the failure mode she is specifically watching for:

> "This product fails the same way my previous investment failed -- strong acquisition, weak retention, because the user's emotional intensity (thesis deadline panic) is time-bounded and the product's value proposition does not survive beyond the deadline."

The thesis-crisis student subscribes in month one, submits in month three, and churns in month four. If this is the dominant user behavior, LTV cannot support CAC at scale.

**The answer visible in the copies:** Learn Mode's mastery arc ("by your second paper, you will not need it the same way; by your third, you will be explaining it to your juniors") is the retention mechanism. Copy D's compound advantage argument is the intellectual framing. But no copy provides evidence that this retention mechanism is working -- no DAU/WAU data, no month-2/month-3 retention figures, no cohort analysis showing that users who complete one paper start another.

**Recommendation:** The landing page needs at least one data point showing that ScholarSync users return for subsequent papers. Even a directional claim -- "68% of users who complete their first paper start a second project within 90 days" -- would address this concern. If this data does not exist yet, building the instrumentation to capture it should be a product priority.

### Warning 4: What Every Persona Wanted but No Copy Provided

Across all seven evaluations, the following gaps appeared repeatedly:

1. **A supervisor dashboard walkthrough:** Every persona above student level asked for this. Professor Priya listed it as the single change that would convert her from "conditional recommendation" to "departmental mandate." No copy showed the dashboard in action.

2. **Publication outcome data from departments:** Dr. Sunita said it plainly: "Give me one real department's numbers, and I sign." No copy provided institutional outcome data. Even a single case study -- Department X went from Y publications/year to Z -- would close this gap.

3. **Data security and privacy statement:** Every institutional buyer asked about data residency, IT compliance, SSO integration, and what happens if the company closes. No copy addressed any of these.

4. **The viva dimension:** Arjun noted that defensibility has an oral component -- what happens when an examiner asks a student to explain their search strategy in front of a committee? Does using ScholarSync make that conversation easier or harder? Only the Learn Mode sections touch on this, and indirectly.

5. **Failure mode acknowledgment:** Vikram wanted to know false positive and false negative rates for AI screening. He wanted to know the limitations, not just the capabilities. "The tool that tells me its limitations earns more trust than the tool that claims it will 'pass' every committee review."

## 6. The Go-to-Market Copy Strategy

### The Landing Page Is One Asset in a Larger Copy Ecosystem

The copy council evaluated landing page variants. But a medical PG student does not discover ScholarSync by navigating to a landing page. They discover it through a WhatsApp forward from a batchmate, a mention from a senior resident, a recommendation from their guide, or a department-level adoption decision. Each of these touchpoints requires its own copy asset, calibrated to a different context, reader state, and character count.

### Complete Copy Asset Map

#### 1. WhatsApp Peer Share Message
**Framework to lean into:** Copy C (Wiebe-Kennedy) + Copy E (Collier)
**Why:** WhatsApp shares must be under 200 characters to avoid being truncated. They must sound like a person talking, not a company marketing. Copy C's "11 PM. 47 browser tabs. A blank Word document" and Copy E's hostel room scene are the most WhatsApp-native moments in the corpus.

**Recommended template:**
> Bro this is literally us right now -- "11 PM, 47 browser tabs, a blank Word document." This tool does the whole thesis workflow in one place. Free plan, no card. [link]

#### 2. Email Sequence (Student Onboarding -- 5 emails)
**Framework to lean into:** Copy A (Schwartz-Halbert) for emails 1-2 (problem agitation), Copy E (Collier) for emails 3-4 (emotional resolution), Copy B (Ogilvy-Hopkins) for email 5 (proof and conversion).
**Why:** Email sequences follow the awareness ladder. Start at Level 4 (problem aware), move to Level 3 (solution aware), close at Level 2 (product aware). This maps to Schwartz-Halbert problem agitation, Collier emotional resolution, and Ogilvy-Hopkins proof.

- **Email 1 (Day 0):** "Nobody taught you the research part." Problem recognition. No product mention.
- **Email 2 (Day 2):** "The Day 4 chaos." The 23-papers-in-six-folders paragraph from Copy A. First mention of ScholarSync.
- **Email 3 (Day 5):** "The victory scene." Copy E's guide saying "This is thorough work." Aspirational state.
- **Email 4 (Day 8):** "How it actually works." Copy B's step-by-step SGLT2 demonstration. Proof.
- **Email 5 (Day 12):** "Less than a textbook." Pricing justification. Free plan CTA.

#### 3. Professor Email Template (for students to forward to their guide)
**Framework to lean into:** Copy B (Ogilvy-Hopkins) + Copy D (Deep Work)
**Why:** Professors need institutional language, ethics defensibility, and intellectual credibility. Copy B's factual proof block and Copy D's Learn Mode philosophy are the most professor-appropriate framings.

**Recommended template:**

ScholarSync is an academic workspace that handles literature search (PubMed, Semantic Scholar, OpenAlex simultaneously), PRISMA-compliant systematic review workflow, and structured academic writing -- with a Learn Mode that explicitly refuses to write for the student, using Socratic questioning to teach methodology instead.

> I thought this might be relevant for the department. There is a free plan with no credit card requirement.
>
> [link]

#### 4. Institutional Pitch Deck Copy
**Framework to lean into:** Copy B (Ogilvy-Hopkins) exclusively
**Why:** Copy B is the only framework that speaks directly to department heads. The comparison table, the Covidence pricing delta, the supervisor dashboard description, and the "catching a methodological error in week two versus week eight" framing are all procurement-ready language.

**Key slides the deck needs:**

**Why:** App Store and Product Hunt descriptions must be scannable, benefit-led, and under 300 words. Copy C's "one workspace where you search, synthesize, write, check, cite, and export -- without switching a single tab" is the most efficient product description in the corpus.

## 8. Pricing Strategy Insights

### What the Personas Revealed About Price Sensitivity

**The student segment (Riya, Rs 65,000/month stipend):**
- Rs 1,000/month is "real money" -- 1.5% of stipend
- The "less than a textbook" comparison landed hardest
- Free plan is essential: "I'd want to use the free plan for a month first"
- The Covidence comparison meant nothing -- she has never heard of Covidence
- Career value framing works: "Against the career value of a single indexed publication, Rs 3,000 is not a calculation worth making" -- this math landed

**The senior resident segment (Arjun, Rs 85,000/month):**
- Rs 2,000/month for Pro is "serious but not unreasonable" -- 2.4% of income
- Covidence comparison is the decisive anchor: Rs 2,000 vs. Rs 13,500
- "Professional investment, not a consumer subscription" framing is exactly right
- Would not subscribe based on copy alone -- needs to trial first

**The post-doc segment (Vikram, currently paying for Covidence):**
- Price is not the primary concern -- defensibility to peer review is
- The Covidence replacement argument is compelling if the methodology holds up
- "I would not cancel Covidence until I had used this for one complete review and successfully defended the methodology"

**The department head segment (Sunita, Rs 5-8 lakh annual budget):**
- Needs cost-per-publication math, not individual subscription framing
- "30 students x Rs 18,000/year = Rs 5.4 lakhs" -- this is the number she needs
- Will not sign without institutional pricing on the page (even a range)
- Wants to compare to the cost of one missed NAAC publication target

### The Institutional vs. Individual Pricing Tension

The council revealed a classic bottom-up SaaS pricing dynamic:

**Individual subscription (Rs 1,000-2,000/month)** is the acquisition mechanism. Students discover the product, use it for their thesis, and become advocates. The pricing must feel accessible at stipend-level income. The "less than a textbook" anchor is the right frame.

**Institutional licensing (custom pricing)** is the revenue mechanism. Department heads buy multi-seat licenses. The pricing must be justifiable as cost-per-publication ROI. The Covidence-replacement frame is the right anchor.

**The gap:** No copy currently bridges these two worlds. The student who loves the product has no language to advocate for institutional adoption. The department head who sees the potential has no pricing to evaluate. The landing page needs a clear institutional section with at least a pricing range (e.g., "Institutional plans start at Rs X per seat per year for departments of 20+") and a structured ROI calculator or case study.

### Recommended Pricing Communication Approach

1. **For student copy:** Lead with "Start free. No credit card." Introduce paid pricing only after the value stack is built. Use the "less than a textbook" anchor. Never mention Covidence -- students do not know what it is.

2. **For professional copy:** Lead with the Covidence comparison using verified pricing. Frame as "professional investment." Emphasize the stack replacement value: "Rs 2,000/month replaces Covidence + Zotero + manual PRISMA work + reference formatting time."

3. **For institutional copy:** Lead with cost-per-publication math. Provide at least a pricing range. Include an ROI framework: "If ScholarSync increases your department's indexed publications by even 2 per year, the per-publication cost of the license is Rs X." Invite contact for custom pricing but do not make "contact us" the only path -- it signals a sales conversation, not a product evaluation, and department heads in government medical colleges do not want to be sold to.

4. **For investor/acquirer audiences:** The pricing story is the wedge narrative. Rs 1,000/month in a market of 75,000+ mandatory research-producing students, expanding to institutional licenses, with a Covidence-displacement thesis for advanced users. The unit economics story writes itself if retention holds past month three.

## Summary: The Strategic Picture

ScholarSync has a product that seven independent evaluators -- spanning the full stakeholder spectrum from student to acquirer -- found genuinely differentiated. The product-market fit signals are strong. The technical depth is real. The India cultural calibration is a defensible moat.

The copy council revealed that **the product is ahead of the copy.** The product has PRISMA compliance, Learn Mode, triple-agent screening, and a 71-table schema. The copy has not yet fully translated these assets into the institutional sales language that closes department-level deals, or the emotional precision that converts crisis-mode students on mobile at 11 PM.

The path forward is clear:

1. **Build one landing page that serves both tiers** -- emotional hero for students, scrollable proof section for professionals, dedicated institutional section for department heads.
2. **Fix the factual errors** -- Covidence pricing, the "methodologically equivalent" overclaim, the "it will pass" guarantee.
3. **Address the humanisation contradiction** before any institutional conversation.
4. **Build the copy ecosystem** -- WhatsApp templates, professor email templates, onboarding flow copy -- because the landing page is where conversion happens, but it is not where discovery happens.
5. **Instrument retention** -- the VC's concern about post-thesis churn is the existential business question, and the answer determines whether this is a Rs 100 crore business or a Rs 10 crore one.

The product is built. The market is starving. The copy needs to close the gap between the two.
