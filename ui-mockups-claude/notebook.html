What are the key differences between SpCas9-HF1 and Prime Editing in terms of off-target reduction?

These are fundamentally different approaches to the same problem. Let me break this down:

SpCas9-HF1

Works by reducing non-specific contacts between the Cas9 protein and DNA. Slaymaker et al. engineered four key residues (N497A, R661A, Q695A, Q926A) to create an “energy barrier” that prevents off-target cleavage while preserving on-target activity.

Source: Slaymaker et al., Nature 2023, p. 421–427

Prime Editing

A completely different mechanism. Uses a Cas9 nickase fused with reverse transcriptase. Rather than creating double-strand breaks, it directly writes new genetic information. This eliminates DSBs entirely, which is the root cause of most off-target mutations.

Source: Anzalone et al., Nature 2021, p. 89–103

The critical distinction: SpCas9-HF1 reduces off-target cutting, while Prime Editing avoids the mechanism that causes off-target damage in the first place. However, Prime Editing has lower efficiency for large insertions.

This raises an interesting question: If Prime Editing avoids DSBs entirely, why hasn't it replaced Cas9-based approaches in clinical trials? What trade-offs might explain this?

R

Good question. Is it an efficiency problem or a delivery problem?

Both, actually, and your instinct to separate them is correct. The literature reveals two distinct bottlenecks:

Efficiency: Prime Editing currently achieves 10–50% editing rates vs. 70–90% for optimized Cas9. For therapeutic applications requiring high correction rates (like sickle cell), this gap is clinically significant.

Delivery: The prime editor construct is ~6.3kb — too large for AAV vectors (4.7kb capacity). This limits in vivo delivery to lipid nanoparticles, which have their own tissue-targeting challenges.

Consider this for your review: How do these practical constraints affect the timeline for Prime Editing in clinical settings? Chen & Liu (2023) address this directly.
